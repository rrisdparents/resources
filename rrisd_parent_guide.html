Your Legal Foundation
Texas Law Guarantees These Rights

These rights exist in Texas statute and apply to every parent in every Texas public school district, including Round Rock ISD. Know them before you walk into any meeting.

⚠ What RRISD May Not Tell You

Round Rock ISD voted on February 19, 2026 to adopt a District of Innovation exemption from Texas Education Code Chapter 26A — the law that specifically protects your grievance rights, including the 60-day filing window and the requirement that the district issue a decision on the merits of your complaint. This exemption was bundled in a consent agenda with routine items. The district is not required to proactively inform you of this. You found out now.

  • The DOI exemption must be publicly posted to be legally effective — verify it is posted before accepting any timeliness denial
  • Even with a DOI exemption, Texas law prohibits exempting mandatory Board duties, including the right to a grievance hearing
  • If you tried to resolve your concern informally first, you may have additional time to file
📋

Right to a Formal Grievance

Every Texas school board must adopt a grievance procedure and address every grievance received concerning a violation of your rights, a board policy, or a provision of the Education Code.

TEC §26.011(a)
⏱

60-Day Filing Window

Under Chapter 26A, you have 60 days from when you learned of the issue to file a grievance. If you attempted informal resolution first, this extends to 90 days — or 30 days from when the district provided filing information.

TEC §26A.002(1)(A)
⚖️

Right to a Merits Decision

The district must issue a decision on the merits of your grievance regardless of procedural objections. The district cannot use procedural technicalities to avoid ruling on the substance of your complaint.

TEC §26A.001(e)(9)
🎧

Right to a Hearing

The district must hold a grievance hearing within 10 days of your filing. You are entitled to at least 5 business days notice before the hearing. Written decisions must follow within 20 days after the hearing.

TEC §26A.001(b)
📁

Right to Supplement Your Record

You may supplement your grievance record with additional documents or claims at any point in the process. The record is not closed until a final Board decision is issued.

TEC §26A.001(e)(5)
🏛

Right to Appeal to TEA

After exhausting the local grievance process, you may appeal the Board's final decision to the TEA Commissioner of Education within 45 calendar days of the final decision.

TEC §7.057
📂

Right to Public Records

You have the right to request all emails, internal communications, and documents related to your child's case. School districts are explicitly named as governmental bodies subject to the Texas Public Information Act.

Tex. Gov't Code §552.003(1)(A)(v)
🔔

Right to Notice of Appeal Rights

When a written decision affecting your child's academic record is communicated to you, that communication is legally required to provide notice of your right to appeal and an explanation of the appeal process — including the process for a final appeal before the Board of Trustees. A filing deadline cannot begin to run from a communication that violated this statutory requirement. If a teacher emails you saying a grade is final without providing any notice of your appeal rights, that email does not start the clock.

TEC §26 — Parents' Bill of Rights
Step-by-Step Workflow
The RRISD Grievance Process

Follow this sequence carefully. Each step has consequences for the next. Missing a deadline or filing at the wrong level can be used against you — even if you were never told the rules.

1
Act Immediately

Document Everything First

Before filing anything, preserve every piece of evidence: emails, grades, portal screenshots, text messages, voicemails, and notes from in-person conversations. Write down dates, times, names, and exact words spoken. Courts and hearing officers rely on contemporaneous records.

Critical: If you record a phone call or meeting, note the date and time of the recording and keep it in a secure location separate from your primary devices. Texas is a one-party consent state for recordings.
2
Within 15–60 Days of Learning of the Issue

Attempt Informal Resolution (But Track It)

Contact the teacher, then the principal, then the assistant superintendent in writing. Always use email so you have a timestamp. If you meet in person, follow up with an email summarizing what was said. Keep every response — and note when you receive no response at all.

Warning: Informal resolution attempts may extend your filing deadline under TEC §26A.002(1)(A)(ii) — but only if documented. The district will not tell you this. Every unanswered email is evidence of their failure to provide notice of your rights.
3
File As Soon As Informal Fails

File Your Level I Grievance — In Writing

File your Level I complaint using the official RRISD form, addressed to the principal or supervisor. State all remedies you are seeking — they cannot be changed at a later level. Include all supporting documents. File via email AND hand delivery so you have proof of receipt. CC the Board of Trustees and the Superintendent.

Critical Rule: The RRISD complaint form states: "INCLUDE ALL REMEDIES SOUGHT. REMEDIES MAY NOT BE AMENDED AT A DIFFERENT LEVEL." List every single thing you want the district to do in your initial filing. Do not assume you can add remedies later.
4
Within 10 Days of Filing

Level I Hearing

A hearing must be held within 10 days of your Level I filing. You are entitled to at least 5 business days notice. Bring all evidence. If the decision does not address all of your allegations, note that omission — it is a procedural deficiency you can raise on appeal.

Tip: Record the hearing if possible. If the district records it, request a copy immediately after. Review the written decision carefully — any allegation the hearing officer acknowledges but fails to rule on is an incomplete decision and grounds for appeal.
5
Within 5–20 Days of Level I Decision

Level II Appeal to the Superintendent

If you are not satisfied with the Level I decision, appeal in writing to the Superintendent's designee. Under RRISD's policy, a timeliness denial may be appealed within 5 days. File immediately — do not wait. Send to RRISD Legal Services and request written confirmation of receipt and routing.

Tip: File supplemental arguments separately as needed. You can supplement the record with new arguments even after filing the main appeal, as long as the hearing has not yet been held.
6
Per Board Policy FNG (Local)

Level III — Appeal to the Board of Trustees

If Level II is denied, you may appeal to the full RRISD Board of Trustees. This is your final local remedy. The Board's decision is the official district position and is the record that TEA and the Commissioner will review on any further appeal.

Note: The Board's decision must be a written decision. If the Board fails to issue a written decision, that is itself a procedural violation you can raise with TEA.
7
Within 45 Days of Board Decision

TEA Commissioner Appeal

After the Board's final decision, you may file a Petition for Review with the TEA Commissioner of Education within 45 calendar days. This is a state-level appeal that can result in the Commissioner ordering the district to comply.

Parallel Track: You can and should file a TEA General Education Complaint simultaneously — you do not need to wait for the local process to conclude. TEA can investigate DOI compliance, posting violations, and systemic grievance procedure failures independently.

Practical Guidance
What They Won't Tell You — But You Need to Know

These are the tactics, patterns, and pitfalls that parents encounter when navigating the RRISD grievance process. Learn them before your first meeting.

✉️

Always Use Email — Never Just Phone

Verbal promises, commitments, and representations made by administrators have no evidentiary value. Every conversation should be followed by an email summarizing what was said. An unanswered email is more valuable than a phone call.

📸

Screenshot District Websites With Timestamps

District policies, DOI plans, and board meeting agendas can be changed after the fact. Screenshot and timestamp any web page that is relevant to your complaint. Take multiple screenshots over time to document changes.

📌

State All Remedies in Your Initial Filing

RRISD's complaint form explicitly states that remedies cannot be changed at a later level. Think carefully before filing. List every specific action you want the district to take. A missed remedy in the initial filing cannot be added later.

🔇

Silence Is Not Neutral

When an administrator fails to respond to your email or concern, document that silence. An administrator's failure to respond to a parent's written concern — especially about a grading or disciplinary decision — is itself relevant to any timeliness argument.

🎙️

Texas Is One-Party Consent for Recordings

In Texas, you may legally record a phone call or in-person meeting you are participating in without informing the other party. If an administrator makes a representation or promise, recording it provides irrefutable evidence. Keep the recording in a secure location.

⚠️

Informal Resolution Attempts Extend Your Deadline

Under TEC §26A.002, if you engaged in informal attempts to resolve the grievance, you may have up to 90 days to file, or 30 days from the date the district provided you filing information — whichever is later. Document every informal contact.

📋

Request Board Policy FNG (Local) in Writing

RRISD is required to provide you with its Board policies. Email legal_services@roundrockisd.org and request a copy of Board Policy FNG (Local) — the governing document for parent complaints. Read it before filing anything.

🔍

Check the DOI Plan for Chapter 26A Exemption

Go to roundrockisd.org and locate the District of Innovation plan. Verify whether Chapter 26A is listed as an exemption and check the date it was posted. If it was added after you raised your concerns, that timing matters.

📬

CC the Board of Trustees and Superintendent

When filing a grievance, CC both rrisd_board@roundrockisd.org and Board_of_Trustees@roundrockisd.org, as well as the Superintendent. This creates a record that the highest levels of district leadership were aware of your complaint from the start.

🏛️

File With TEA Simultaneously — Don't Wait

You do not need to exhaust the local grievance process before filing a TEA General Complaint about DOI posting violations, failure to follow grievance procedures, or systemic compliance issues. File both tracks simultaneously.

📁

File a TPIA Request for Internal Emails

You have the right to request all internal communications between administrators and Legal Services regarding your complaint. File a Texas Public Information Act request immediately. Once filed, the district cannot legally destroy any responsive records.

⚖️

Incomplete Decisions Are Appealable

If a hearing officer acknowledges your allegation in the grievance section of their decision but fails to rule on it in the conclusion, that is a procedural deficiency. You can raise this at Level II and demand the omitted allegation be adjudicated on the merits.

Documented Pattern of Conduct
Known RRISD Tactics Used Against Parents

The following tactics have been documented in at least one parent's grievance proceedings against Round Rock ISD. They are described here without identifying the parent involved. If you recognize any of these patterns, you are not alone — and knowing they exist is your first line of defense.

🚨 This Section Is Based on a Real Case

Every tactic listed below was documented in a formal grievance proceeding involving RRISD during the 2025–2026 school year. Evidence includes recorded phone calls, timestamped emails, screenshots, and written decisions from RRISD administrators. This is not speculation. These things happened.

Let's be direct about what RRISD's grievance process is actually designed to do.

Their goal is not to help you. Their goal is to make sure your complaint never reaches Level Three — where the full Board of Trustees would have to address it publicly on the record. Every tactic below serves that single purpose: to exhaust you, confuse you, and run out the clock before you get there.

They will ignore your emails and then cite those same emails against you. They will tell you to refile and then deny the refiling. They will promise hearings on recorded calls and deny making any promise. They will acknowledge your allegations in a written decision and then quietly drop them in the conclusion. They will cite policies at hearings that were not legally posted at the time. They will bury the removal of your rights in a consent agenda alongside field turf approvals and door hardware so that no one notices.

None of this is accidental. The process is structured to make parents give up at Level One or Level Two — when they are tired, confused, and don't know what they don't know. The district has attorneys. You have the right to know what they know. That is what this page is for.

⚠ DO NOT WITHDRAW ANY GRIEVANCE WITHOUT WRITTEN CONFIRMATION THAT YOUR REFILING WILL BE ACCEPTED. DO NOT MISS A DEADLINE BECAUSE AN ADMINISTRATOR TOLD YOU THEY WOULD HANDLE IT. GET EVERYTHING IN WRITING. RECORD EVERY CALL.

🚫

Tactic #1: Ignoring Parent Emails Entirely — Then Using Them Against You Later

A parent sent a detailed written email to an RRISD administrator raising concerns about a teacher's conduct and a grading dispute. The administrator never responded — not in days, not in weeks, not at all. Months later, when the parent filed a formal grievance, the district cited that same email as proof that the parent knew about the issue and had missed the filing deadline. The administrator's complete failure to respond to the email — including failure to provide any notice of the parent's right to appeal or information about the grievance process — was entirely omitted from the district's analysis.

What to do: Every email you send is potential evidence in both directions. If you don't get a response within 5 business days, send a follow-up in writing. Document the silence explicitly: "As of [date] I have received no response to my [date] email."
🎭

Tactic #2: Making Promises on a Recorded Call — Then Denying It

A principal called a parent directly, explicitly stated that a Level I grievance hearing would be scheduled for a specific unresolved issue, offered specific dates, used the words "grievance hearing" and "grievance process," and asked the parent to confirm via email. The parent complied. The principal then sent a follow-up email — copied to Legal Services — asking when the parent would like to schedule the Level I hearing. Less than 48 hours later, Legal Services denied the complaint as untimely. When the matter came before a hearing officer, the principal claimed he had only been suggesting the parent refile as a procedural matter and denied any intent to promise a hearing. The audio recording of the call directly contradicted this account.

What to do: Record every phone call with an administrator. Texas is a one-party consent state — you do not need to inform them. If an administrator makes any promise or commitment, follow up immediately in writing: "As per our call today, you confirmed [X]. Please confirm by reply email."
🪤

Tactic #3: Instructing a Parent to Refile — Then Denying the Refiling as Untimely

A parent filed a formal grievance covering multiple issues. An administrator instructed the parent to withdraw the active grievance and refile certain issues on a separate form, representing that a Level I hearing would follow. The parent complied in good faith. Within 48 hours — while the administrator's scheduling email was still fresh — Legal Services denied the refiling as untimely under a policy the parent had never been shown. The administrator later denied any intent to mislead the parent. The parent had given up their standing in the original grievance based entirely on the administrator's instruction and promise.

What to do: Never withdraw an active grievance based on a verbal or even written promise from an administrator unless you have confirmed in writing that the new filing will be accepted and heard. If an administrator tells you to refile, ask them to confirm in writing that the refiling will be treated as timely before you withdraw anything.
🙈

Tactic #4: Never Telling Parents the Grievance Policy Exists — Until It's Too Late

Across multiple contacts spanning months — emails to a VP, an in-person meeting, and phone calls — not a single RRISD administrator ever mentioned the existence of Board Policy FNG (Local), told the parent about the formal grievance process, referenced a filing deadline, or provided any information about how to formally appeal a decision affecting the student's academic record. The parent discovered the grievance process entirely through personal research. When the parent initially filed, they filed what they believed was a Level II because they had already gone through what they understood as Level I — informal discussions with school staff. The very first time any district administrator cited Board Policy FNG (Local) to the parent was in the letter denying their complaint as untimely.

What to do: Do not wait for the district to tell you the rules. Email legal_services@roundrockisd.org immediately and request a copy of Board Policy FNG (Local). If you have already received a decision you disagree with, assume a deadline is running and file immediately while you research.
🗂️

Tactic #5: Acknowledging an Allegation in a Decision — Then Quietly Dropping It

A parent's Level I grievance included two distinct allegations — a grading dispute and professional misconduct. The principal's written decision acknowledged both allegations by name in the grievance section. The conclusion then addressed only the three specific remedies listed and said nothing further about the professional misconduct allegation. No finding. No denial. No rationale. The allegation appeared on page one of the decision and disappeared entirely from the conclusion. This is not an accident — it is a technique for appearing to process a complaint while avoiding accountability on the most serious allegation.

What to do: Read every decision carefully. Compare the allegations listed at the top against the rulings in the conclusion. If any allegation is acknowledged but not ruled on, that is a procedural deficiency you must raise in your Level II appeal. Demand that the Level II officer rule on every allegation that Level I abandoned.
🌐

Tactic #6: Citing a Policy at a Hearing That Wasn't Posted Until Three Days Later

At a formal Level I hearing in May 2026, RRISD's General Counsel cited the district's District of Innovation plan exemption from Texas Education Code Chapter 26A as the basis for denying a parent's grievance rights. Timestamped screenshots captured by the parent during the hearing itself — at approximately 6:20 PM — confirmed that the Chapter 26A exemption was not present in the DOI plan as posted on the RRISD website at that time. The plan was updated on the district's website three days after the hearing to include the Chapter 26A exemption. The parent has before-and-after screenshots documenting this change.

What to do: Screenshot the RRISD website — especially the DOI plan at roundrockisd.org — before, during, and after any hearing. Use a tool that captures the full URL and timestamp. Under TEC §12A.0071, a DOI exemption that is not publicly posted has no legal force. An exemption cited at a hearing that was not posted at the time of the hearing cannot be enforced against you.
📝

Tactic #7: A Teacher Making a Decision That Contradicts Her Own Email

A teacher assigned a student a zero and communicated to the parent in writing that the grade was final with no opportunity to remedy. Months later, when the matter was formally raised, the teacher told administration she had offered the student a rewrite opportunity that the student simply failed to complete. This claim was directly contradicted by the teacher's own written email from the time of the decision. No rewrite was ever offered. The family would have accepted one. This type of post-hoc fabrication — constructing a justification after the fact when a grade is challenged — leaves a parent with no remedy if they don't have the original email preserved.

What to do: Save every email from every teacher and administrator in a folder outside your primary email client. Save them as PDFs with timestamps. If a teacher tells you a grade is final, that email is evidence. If they later claim they offered you options you never received, that email proves they are lying.
🔧

Tactic #8: Citing the Wrong Policy in a Formal Decision

A formal Level I grievance decision issued by an RRISD hearing officer opened by citing Board Policy DGBA (Local) as the governing standard. DGBA (Local) is the employee grievance policy. The correct policy for a parent complaint is FNG (Local). The decision then switched to FNG (Local) in the conclusion without acknowledging the error. A decision that applies the wrong policy at the outset is substantively deficient. This type of error — whether intentional or not — can confuse parents about their rights and make it harder to identify the correct appeal procedures.

What to do: When you receive any written decision, check which policy it cites. For parent complaints, the applicable policy is FNG (Local) — not DGBA (employee complaints) or GF (community member complaints). If the wrong policy is cited, raise that deficiency explicitly in your appeal.
🎯

Tactic #9: Bundling Rights Removal in a Consent Agenda

RRISD's vote to exempt itself from Texas Education Code Chapter 26A — the law that protects your right to a 60-day grievance window and a merits decision — was passed on February 19, 2026 as part of a consent agenda. Consent agendas are bundles of routine items voted on together without individual discussion. This particular vote was bundled with items including field turf approvals, door hardware, and school renovation items. There was no separate public deliberation on the removal of parental grievance rights. The exemption also was not publicly posted on the district website until 63 days after the required posting deadline — and was only added after a parent caught its absence at a formal hearing.

What to do: Attend or watch Board meetings. Review consent agendas before they are voted on — RRISD is required to post them in advance. If a consent agenda item affects parental rights, request that it be pulled for separate discussion. File a TEA complaint if parental rights are modified through a consent agenda without public deliberation.
⚖️

Tactic #10: Sending a "Grade Is Final" Email With No Notice of Your Right to Appeal — Then Using That Email to Start the Deadline Clock

A teacher emailed a parent to communicate that a student's grade was final — a zero based on an accusation of academic dishonesty that dropped the student's semester grade from an A to a B. The email stated the decision was final and provided no further information. It did not identify the policy under which the decision was made. It did not inform the parent of any right to dispute or appeal the grade. It did not reference the grievance process, a filing deadline, or any procedure for challenging the decision. Months later, when the parent filed a formal grievance, the district cited that same email as proof that the parent had known about the issue since that date — and therefore missed the 15-day filing deadline.

This tactic has a specific statutory answer. Texas Education Code §26 — the Parents' Bill of Rights — requires that when a written decision affecting a student's academic record is communicated to a parent, that written communication must provide notice of the student's ability to appeal the decision and an explanation of the appeal process, including the process for a final appeal before the Board of Trustees. The teacher's email violated this requirement. A filing deadline cannot legally begin to run from a communication that violated the statute requiring notice of that very deadline. The district cannot use a non-compliant communication to start a clock the law required them to disclose in that same communication.

What to do: If you receive any written communication from a teacher or administrator stating that a grade, placement, or disciplinary decision is "final," check whether it includes notice of your right to appeal and the appeal process. If it does not, cite TEC §26 in every filing. The absence of that required notice means the deadline did not begin to run on the date of that communication. Raise this argument explicitly in your Level I filing, your Level II appeal, and your TEA complaint.

If you have experienced any of these tactics, you are not alone.

File a TEA General Education Complaint at ComplaintsManagement@tea.texas.gov. Document everything. Request internal emails through a TPIA request. Appeal every denial. The district counts on parents giving up. Don't.

Texas Public Information Act
How to Request Internal District Records

Round Rock ISD is explicitly named as a governmental body under Tex. Gov't Code §552.003(1)(A)(v). You have the right to request emails, internal communications, and policy documents — and they cannot ask you why.

What You Can Request

Under the Texas Public Information Act, you can request any record created or maintained in connection with official district business, including:

  • All emails between administrators (principal, VP, Legal Services) referencing your child or your complaint
  • Internal communications about the District of Innovation plan amendment and any legal analysis supporting it
  • Board meeting materials, supporting documents, and communications related to specific consent agenda items
  • Any legal opinions or memoranda about Chapter 26A or the parental grievance policy
  • Communications between administrators and Legal Services about parent grievances or Chapter 26A from any date range

Sample TPIA Request Language

Pursuant to Texas Government Code §552.021 and §552.003(1)(A)(v), which expressly defines a school district board of trustees as a governmental body subject to the Public Information Act, I hereby request copies of the following public records: 1. All emails, messages, or communications to, from, or copying [Administrator Name] referencing [your name] or [student name] between [date range]. 2. All emails or communications between Legal Services and any district administrator referencing parent grievances, Chapter 26A, or the district's parental grievance policy between [date range]. 3. All supporting materials, legal opinions, or background documents prepared in connection with any District of Innovation plan amendment, including any analysis of the legal authority for exempting Chapter 26A. I request records in electronic format via email. Please notify me in advance if costs will exceed $40. Pursuant to Tex. Gov't Code §552.228, I request a written response within 10 business days. If any records are withheld, please identify each withheld record and the specific exception claimed, and submit a request for an AG ruling as required by law.

Send to: legal_services@roundrockisd.org | Subject: "Public Information Request Pursuant to Texas Public Information Act" | Keep a copy of your email with the send timestamp.

State-Level Escalation
Filing a TEA General Education Complaint

TEA cannot overturn a local grievance decision directly — but it CAN investigate whether the district's grievance procedures comply with state law, whether DOI posting requirements were met, and whether systemic violations occurred.

📝

What TEA Can Investigate

DOI plan posting violations, failure to follow required grievance procedures, failure to issue merits decisions, and systemic patterns of non-compliance with state education law.

⏰

TEA Response Timeline

TEA must provide a written response within 60 days of receiving your complaint. They may request additional information within 15 days — respond promptly or your complaint may be closed.

📎

What to Include

All grievance decisions received, timestamped screenshots of district website, the recording of any administrator promises, email chains, and a clear timeline of events with dates.

🔗

File Directly — Not Via CC

TEA does not respond to CC'd correspondence. All TEA filings must go directly to ComplaintsManagement@tea.texas.gov with your reference number in the subject line.

🏛️

Commissioner Appeal — TEC §7.057

After the Board's final decision, you may file a Petition for Review with the TEA Commissioner within 45 days. The Commissioner can order the district to comply. File within 45 days of the announced decision — not the mailed decision.

🔄

Both Tracks Simultaneously

You do not need to exhaust local remedies before filing with TEA on compliance issues. Run both the local grievance process and the TEA complaint simultaneously. They reinforce each other.
